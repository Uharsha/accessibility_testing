1. Detailed Explanation of "Perceivable" in Web Accessibility

The principle of Perceivable is the first guideline of the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) and ensures that all users can perceive the content of a website or application. This principle focuses on making information and user interface components available to the senses of sight, sound, and touch. Core Components of Perceivable 1. Text Alternatives (Guideline 1.1)
o Provide text alternatives for any non-text content (e.g., images, charts, audio).
o Text alternatives ensure that assistive technologies like screen readers can convey the information to visually impaired users.
Examples:
o Good Practice: Adding alt text for an image, such as .
o Bad Practice: Using without describing the chart.
2. Time-Based Media (Guideline 1.2)
o Provide alternatives for audio and video content, such as captions, transcripts, and audio descriptions.
o This ensures content is accessible to people who are deaf, hard of hearing, blind, or visually impaired.
Examples:
o Good Practice: Including captions for a YouTube video explaining a product.
o Bad Practice: Releasing a podcast without a transcript.
3. Adaptable Content (Guideline 1.3)
o Create content that can be presented in different ways without losing meaning.
o Use semantic HTML to define headings, lists, and tables to ensure assistive technologies can interpret the content structure correctly.
Examples:
o Good Practice: Using tags for headings and for lists.
o Bad Practice: Using bold text for headings without actual tags.
4. Distinguishable (Guideline 1.4)
o Make content easy to see and hear, including foreground and background contrast, text size, and audio control.
Examples:
o Good Practice: Ensuring a minimum color contrast ratio of 4.5:1 between text and background colors.
o Bad Practice: Using light gray text on a white background.

Practical Examples of Perceivable in Action


1. Images and Graphics:
o Scenario: An e-commerce site with product images.
 Perceivable: Include descriptive alt text like "Black leather boots with a 2-inch heel."
 Not Perceivable: Using alt text like "image123.jpg" or leaving it empty.

o Scenario: A company training video.
 Perceivable: Adding captions for dialogue and sound effects and providing a text transcript.
 Not Perceivable: Relying solely on the video’s visuals and audio without alternatives.
